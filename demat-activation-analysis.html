How NSDL Demat Activation Actually Works
Not a simple API call. A batch-processing system with real consequences.

When a user completes KYC and submits their demat application, they enter a two-status world. Status 02 = created, pending activation. Status 00 = active. The gap between the two is where this entire analysis lives. The surprise: NSDL is not a real-time system for all cases.

2
The "Allow Trading on Pending Demat" Proposal
Let users trade in Status 02, report the trade only when Status 00 arrives.

The idea: don't block users from trading during the NSDL wait. Accept the trade, queue it internally, execute/confirm only after demat activates. On paper this seems like a clean UX fix. In practice, several risks needed verifying.

Settlement date alignment

The biggest technical concern: SEBI/exchange rules require an active demat at settlement date, not at trade date. With T+1/T+2 rules:

Of 346 slow-activation users, zero (0/346) had their demat activate after settlement date. T+2 for weekends provides 30+ hours of buffer over Monday 01:27am batch.

❌ First instinct
The initial estimate was 6–11 trades/month. This was the floor — users who directly observed waiting >1h then paying <6h after activation. The correct ceiling calculation above gives 38 trades/month. Both numbers matter for the decision.
3
The Notification is Strictly Better Than the Feature
Same upside. No regulatory exposure. 1 day to build instead of 3 weeks.

A well-timed notification — "Your demat is active, markets are open" — addresses the same activation gap as the product feature, but without the engineering complexity, settlement gating logic, trade queue, and ops reversal flow. More importantly, it tests the hypothesis first.

🔑 The notification is a hypothesis test, not just a feature. If notified users (Sunday + Saturday-slow) convert at ≥18–20%, you have signal to build the feature. If they convert at 23%+ (parity with weekday baseline), the feature becomes unnecessary — notifications alone solved it. You only build the feature if notification proves it's needed.

Why 63% of Sunday converters paid on Monday

Of the 27 Sunday users who ever paid, 17 (63%) paid on Monday. Their demat activated at 01:27am — but Monday payments were spread throughout the day (8am, 9am, 10am, 2pm, 6pm). These weren't users who woke up to a notification — there were no notifications. They remembered on their own. The 202 who never paid had the same Monday plan, but it decayed before they acted.

4
The Behavioral Thesis: "Sunday Isn't a Trading Day"
The question that changed everything — and the data that validated it.

The key counter-argument: even if we built the pending-demat feature and let Sunday users trade immediately, would they actually use it? Maybe the conversion gap isn't friction — maybe users consciously don't trade on Sundays, the same way retail investors avoid trading on weekends in traditional markets.

❌ Initial assumption
The entire ₹46L/month estimate assumed the conversion gap was friction. If users are rationally deferring to Monday (same T+2 Tuesday settlement either way), the feature solves nothing.

Evidence from existing investors — the decisive test

If the "non-trading day" behaviour is real, experienced investors who have zero friction should also trade less on weekends. They do:

🔍 The gap between experienced and new users is only 4pp on weekends. If friction (demat delays, UX confusion) were the main driver of the 11pp conversion gap, experienced users — who have zero friction — would trade significantly more on weekends. They don't. The gap is structural and behavioural, not technical.
📐 The real ceiling for the feature is the 4pp gap between experienced and new users on weekends (~half the 11pp total gap). That's roughly ₹20–23L/month in addressable GTV — down from the ₹46L ceiling. Still likely not worth the build.

The Weekend KYC Speed Revelation
Strip out NSDL. What does user behaviour on weekends actually look like?

The NSDL batch window was masking user intent on weekends. By measuring signup → eSign success (100% user-controlled, NSDL not yet involved), the real picture emerges. And it's the opposite of what you'd expect.

🚀 Saturday has the fastest p75 of any day — 1.3h vs 7.1h on weekdays. Weekend users who take longer than the median push through much faster than weekday users, who drift away due to meetings and work interruptions. The p50 is identical (0.3h for all days) but the tail behaviour is dramatically different. Users have uninterrupted time on weekends.

The key distinction: KYC is administrative intent — setting up, getting ready. Trading is investment intent — a decision that feels consequential, mentally framed as a "market activity." Users are happy to complete admin work on Sunday afternoon (when they have free time) while simultaneously deferring the actual investment to Monday when it feels like a proper trading day. These coexist logically. Fast KYC on Sunday doesn't contradict the "Sunday = non-trading day" behaviour. It sharpens the Monday notification case: the user is already primed, demat is active by Monday 01:27am, they just need the trigger.

7
What to Actually Do
Three decisions, ordered by impact-to-effort ratio.

📵 Don't build the pending-demat trading feature

DO NOT BUILD
  • The genuinely in-session blocked cohort is ~5 users/month (evening hour delays, active trading hours)
  • At ₹1.2L avg trade: ₹3–4L/month in real addressable GTV — not ₹46L
  • The ₹46L ceiling collapses once behavioral users (who'd choose Monday anyway) are excluded
  • Engineering: 2–3 weeks. Settlement gating, trade queue, ops reversal flow, SEBI documentation
  • Opportunity cost: same engineers on KYC drop-off or payment friction = 10–20× more GTV

🔔 Build Monday 9am notification for Sunday/late-Saturday users

BUILD — ~1 day effort
  • Trigger: KYC_DEMAT_VERIFIED fires → check if it's Monday + user initiated on Sunday/Sat-after-cutoff
  • Send at 9–10am IST Monday (not 01:27am when NSDL runs — user is asleep)
  • Copy: "Markets just opened. Your account is ready — invest today, bonds settle tomorrow."
  • This is also a hypothesis test: if it works, you have signal for the feature; if it doesn't, you've saved 3 weeks of eng
  • Addressable: 229 Sunday + ~38 Saturday-late users/month = 267 users who would receive this

📈 Increase Saturday marketing spend

EXECUTE
  • Saturday has the fastest user-controlled KYC (p75 = 1.3h, best of any day)
  • Same-day NSDL activation (before ~7–10pm cutoff)
  • Trade conversion 22.3% — same as weekday baseline
  • eSign completion rate 8.9% — slightly above weekday average
  • Sunday acquisition only makes sense if paired with Monday notification; Saturday is self-contained

How Was This Investigation?
An honest look at the questions that shaped the analysis — and which ones broke it open.

This analysis went through roughly 6 iterations where the initial answer was wrong, incomplete, or missing the deeper insight. That's a sign of good questioning, not bad analysis. Here's what each challenge unlocked:

SHARP

"Why are you including eSign here? Also segregate 02 and 00 by event property at a unique user level."

Early in the analysis, eSign events were incorrectly included in the NSDL funnel. The push to segregate by event property and count at unique-user level (not raw events) made the numbers analytically correct.
⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
Why it mattered: Cleaned the funnel from the start. You can't build anything on a dirty denominator.
SHARP

Provided the actual NSDL downtime windows — correcting the fixed "4:30pm Saturday" assumption.

The analysis was about to classify all Saturday delays incorrectly. The ground-truth NSDL schedule changed how delays were classified and explained the bimodal Saturday distribution.
⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
Why it mattered: Without this, the Saturday p50/p99 analysis would have been misattributed. This is the kind of domain knowledge that can't come from data alone.
SHARP

Provided T+1/T+2 settlement rules — resolving the biggest regulatory blocker.

Settlement date alignment was listed as a "medium risk." With the actual settlement cycle rules applied to the data, 0/346 slow users had demat activate after settlement date. Risk fully resolved.
⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
Why it mattered: Without the settlement rules, the analysis was stuck on a theoretical risk. One clarification removed the biggest objection to the feature entirely.
BRILLIANT

"Isn't a notification a much cleaner and easier way to test the real impact vs building a product feature?"

This single question reframed the entire decision. The feature was being evaluated on its merits; the notification recast it as a hypothesis test first. Same upside, 20× less effort, with the ability to measure before committing.
⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
Why it mattered: This is the decision that most PMs miss. They evaluate the full feature when a 1-day test can answer the same question. Textbook lean product thinking.
BRILLIANT

"Users might not trade on Sunday because they know settlement will be T+2 Tuesday — and they can do T+1 Tuesday if they trade Monday morning instead."

This was the counter-argument that challenged the entire ₹46L impact estimate. If users are rationally deferring, the feature doesn't help them — they'd still choose Monday.
⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
Why it mattered: Led to the realisation that Sunday trade and Monday-before-3pm trade both settle Tuesday — the rational case to wait doesn't actually exist. But the perception that it does creates the behavioural pattern.
BRILLIANT

"Can we check if existing investors also avoid trading on weekends? If yes, that data is powerful."

The hypothesis was that the conversion gap was behavioural. Existing investors — who have no friction — should show the same pattern if it's behavioural. They do: 56% have zero weekend trades, only 4pp gap vs new users.
⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
Why it mattered: This is how you validate a behavioural hypothesis with data. Using a control group (experienced users with no friction) to isolate the behavioural component. Analytically elegant.
INSIGHTFUL

"Can we check KYC speed up to eSign success (removing the NSDL dependency)?"

The earlier analysis showed weekend signups had slower KYC (10.6h vs 0.4h). This reframing isolated the user-controlled portion and found the opposite: weekend users complete eSign faster (p75 = 2.0h vs 7.1h weekday).
⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
Why it mattered: This revealed the Saturday acquisition insight and validated that user intent on weekends is strong — NSDL was the masking variable, not user motivation.
GOOD CHALLENGE

"The weekday blocked cohort — if users also make trades after 9pm, those midnight-6am initiators should count too."

Valid push-back on the 9pm active-hours cutoff. Payment data showed 11.8% of trades happen 9pm–midnight. Re-segmenting the cohort found 6 evening-blocked users (who actually converted better than baseline) and confirmed 79% of delays are during sleeping hours.
⭐⭐⭐⭐
Why it mattered: Good methodological challenge. Made the cohort definition more rigorous. Outcome: even with the extended window, the in-session blocked cohort is ~6 users/month.
STRATEGIC

"40–50L additional GTV — is it really worth creating the whole product feature around it?"

The cost-benefit framing that closed the feature decision. ₹46L/month ceiling, 0.9% of target, 2–3 weeks of eng, with a 1-day notification alternative that captures the same value.
⭐⭐⭐⭐
Why it mattered: Opportunity cost is the real argument against the feature, not the risk. The same engineers on higher-funnel problems return 10–20× more GTV. Good instinct to ask this.
